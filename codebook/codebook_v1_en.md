\## Part A: Platform Values (10 codes, replicating Su \& Chan, 2025, ICS)



These ten codes are reproduced verbatim from Su, C. C., \& Chan, N. K. (2025), 

"Assembling platform governance as private ordering in the age of generative AI", 

\*Information, Communication \& Society\*, Table 2. Operational definitions are 

preserved word-for-word; only positive examples, negative examples, and edge 

cases are added by the present author for coding clarity.



\---



\### A1. Power



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform or users can 

govern content and protect their rights.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section asserts who has the authority to 

make decisions about content, accounts, or platform behavior — whether the 

platform itself, users, or both. This includes statements about the 

platform's authority to remove, suspend, or restrict; or statements 

empowering users to challenge platform decisions.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "We reserve the right to remove any content that violates these guidelines."

\- "Users may appeal account suspensions through our review process."

\- "TikTok will determine, in its sole discretion, what content qualifies."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT power)\*\*:

\- "We work to keep our community safe." (this is safety, not power)

\- "Users can choose to make their account private." (this is choice)

\- "We may update these terms from time to time." (procedural, not power)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- A statement that the platform "may" do something often signals power.

\- "Users are responsible for X" can be power if it implies enforcement.



\---



\### A2. Privacy



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which users are empowered to 

manage their personal information, encompassing permissions for data control, 

sharing, and customization.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section discusses how users can control 

their personal information — including data collection, data use, data 

sharing, account visibility settings tied to personal info, or customization 

of privacy settings.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "You can review and delete the personal information you have shared."

\- "We will not disclose your information to third parties without consent."

\- "Users may opt out of personalized advertising."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT privacy)\*\*:

\- "Users can choose to make their account private." (this is choice — about 

&#x20; selecting an option, not about managing personal information)

\- "We protect our community from harmful content." (this is safety)

\- "Do not share other people's private information." (this is about user 

&#x20; behavior toward others, not the user's own personal info — 

&#x20; this is more likely safety)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Statements about cookies, tracking, third-party data sharing → privacy.

\- Statements telling users not to dox others → safety, not privacy.



\---



\### A3. Safety



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which platform allows or 

prohibits users from posting to preserve the well-being of users, the 

platform community, and/or organizations.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section describes content rules, 

prohibitions, or affirmative protections oriented toward preventing harm — 

whether physical, psychological, or social — to users, the community, or 

organizations.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "We do not allow content that depicts or promotes violence."

\- "Content that may harm minors will be removed."

\- "We prohibit hate speech, harassment, and threats."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT safety)\*\*:

\- "We may remove content at our discretion." (this is power)

\- "Users can report harmful content." (this is engagement / a 

&#x20; participation mechanism — though "we will act on reports" would be 

&#x20; power+safety)

\- "Our community values diversity." (this is community)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Most TikTok prohibitions are safety. When in doubt, look at WHY the 

&#x20; prohibition exists — if the answer is "to prevent harm", it's safety.



\---



\### A4. Choice



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which users are free to pick 

options that align with their interests (e.g., opt-in/opt-out).



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section describes mechanisms by which users 

can select among options to customize their platform experience.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "Users can choose to receive notifications via email or app."

\- "You may opt out of data collection for advertising purposes."

\- "Account holders can switch between public and private mode."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT choice)\*\*:

\- "We will determine if your content violates these rules." (this is power)

\- "Your data will be processed lawfully." (this is privacy)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- "Opt-in/opt-out" language is a strong cue for choice.

\- The presence of \*any\* user-selectable option does not automatically make 

&#x20; the section about choice — the section must \*substantively discuss\* the 

&#x20; user's selection autonomy.



\---



\### A5. Community



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform values a 

certain social group characterized by shared practices, communication 

technologies, and intimate relations.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section invokes "the community", "our 

users", "our creators", or similar collective terms in a way that 

constructs them as a coherent social group with shared practices, norms, 

or relations — beyond mere reference to "users".



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "Our community thrives on creativity and authentic expression."

\- "We are committed to building a safe space for our diverse community."

\- "TikTok creators form a global network of storytellers."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT community)\*\*:

\- "Users must comply with these terms." (just refers to users, no 

&#x20; community-building rhetoric)

\- "We do not allow harmful content." (safety)

\- "Personal information is collected when you use our services." (privacy)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- "Community" as the literal title of "Community Guidelines" does not by 

&#x20; itself make a section about community — the section's content must invoke 

&#x20; community-as-collective.

\- The word "community" must appear with positive valence (a group to be 

&#x20; valued/protected/celebrated), not just used neutrally.



\---



\### A6. Engagement



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform allows or 

prohibits interactivity and participation through the platform for certain 

outcomes.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section discusses how users interact with 

or participate on the platform — including posting, commenting, liking, 

sharing, livestreaming, sending gifts, etc. — and what is encouraged or 

allowed about such participation.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "Users may post videos up to 10 minutes in length."

\- "Sending virtual gifts is a way to support your favorite creators."

\- "Comments must remain respectful and on-topic."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT engagement)\*\*:

\- "We will remove content that violates our rules." (power)

\- "Users own the content they create." (intellectual property)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Engagement often co-occurs with safety (when prohibitions on participation 

&#x20; are discussed) — code BOTH in that case.

\- LIVE-related rules about going live, hosting, gifting are typically 

&#x20; engagement.



\---



\### A7. Protection of Intellectual Property



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform establishes 

rules, guidelines, and mechanisms to safeguard the ownership and rights of 

creators and organizations over their content, ideas, and digital assets, 

including prohibiting unauthorized use, distribution, and reproduction.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section discusses copyright, trademark, 

licensing, content ownership, or unauthorized use/reproduction of content.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "We respect the intellectual property rights of creators."

\- "Do not post content that infringes on copyright."

\- "TikTok responds to valid DMCA takedown notices."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT IP)\*\*:

\- "Users own the data they share with us." (this is privacy, not IP — 

&#x20; unless the section specifically frames it as creative ownership)

\- "We may license your content to third parties." (this is more likely 

&#x20; power + privacy + a TOS clause about platform rights)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Content licensing in TOS often blurs IP and power. Code IP if the focus 

&#x20; is on ownership rights; code power if the focus is on platform's 

&#x20; authority to use content.



\---



\### A8. Improvement



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform strives to 

improve its available features and become central actors of private and 

public life.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section describes how the platform is 

enhancing, expanding, or evolving its features, services, or role in 

society.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "We continually improve our recommendation algorithms."

\- "TikTok is investing in new tools for creators."

\- "Our platform now supports longer videos and richer creative formats."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT improvement)\*\*:

\- "Users can post videos." (engagement, just describing existing 

&#x20; functionality)

\- "We may update these terms." (procedural, not improvement)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Forward-looking language ("we will", "we plan to", "we are working on") 

&#x20; often signals improvement.

\- Statements about new policy versions (e.g., "as of March 2024, we have 

&#x20; updated our guidelines") are usually NOT improvement — they're meta-

&#x20; policy statements.



\---



\### A9. Care



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform provides 

information about support for users and outlines how users can seek help.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section directs users toward resources for 

support — whether mental health, safety reporting, customer service, or 

educational materials.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "If you or someone you know is struggling, please contact a crisis 

&#x20; hotline at..."

\- "Visit our Safety Center for tools and guides."

\- "Resources for parents are available at..."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT care)\*\*:

\- "Report content that violates our rules." (engagement / safety, not care 

&#x20; — care is about getting help, not enforcement)

\- "We will respond to your appeals within 7 days." (procedural, not care)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Provision of phone numbers, hotlines, or external resources is a strong 

&#x20; cue for care.

\- Internal report buttons are NOT care — they're enforcement mechanisms.



\---



\### A10. Accountability



\*\*Definition (Su \& Chan, 2025)\*\*: The degree to which a platform or users 

has a mechanism for holding the platform accountable.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section describes formal mechanisms by 

which the platform commits to transparency, audit, appeal, or external 

oversight — and by which users (or regulators) can hold the platform to 

account.



\*\*Positive examples\*\*:

\- "Users may appeal moderation decisions through our review process."

\- "We publish a quarterly transparency report on content removal."

\- "Our policies are reviewed by an independent advisory council."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT accountability)\*\*:

\- "We will remove violating content." (power, not accountability)

\- "Users must follow these rules." (no accountability mechanism)



\*\*Edge cases\*\*:

\- Accountability is about checks on the PLATFORM's behavior, not on user 

&#x20; behavior. "We hold users accountable" is power; "users can hold us 

&#x20; accountable" is accountability.

\- Transparency reports, appeal processes, third-party audits are all 

&#x20; classic accountability signals.

\## Part B: Cultural Heritage Codes (6 codes, original to this study)



These six codes are designed for the present study, drawing on Smith (2006) 

\*Uses of Heritage\* and the empirical observations of Paquienséguy \& Guo 

(2025) on Douyin's intangible cultural heritage practices. Each code 

captures a distinct dimension of how TikTok's policy text governs content 

that performs, references, or commercializes cultural traditions, religion, 

or indigenous identity.



The six codes are designed to be \*\*mutually exclusive in definition\*\* and 

\*\*non-overlapping with the 10 platform values in Part A\*\*. If a section 

discusses authenticity in general (e.g., misinformation), code none of the 

six heritage codes — heritage codes apply only when the cultural content 

context is clearly invoked.



\---



\### B1. Authenticity Claims (Heritage Authenticity)



\*\*Definition\*\*: The platform asserts, evaluates, or governs whether 

cultural, traditional, or religious content is "authentic", "genuine", 

"original", or "real" — invoking notions of cultural truthfulness or 

traditional legitimacy.



\*\*Theoretical anchor\*\*: Smith (2006, \*Uses of Heritage\*) argues that 

institutions implicitly define which cultural performances count as 

"authentic heritage", privileging some forms while marginalizing others.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section evaluates the legitimacy or 

authenticity of cultural performances, traditional practices, religious 

expressions, or claims to ethnic/cultural identity. The text must engage 

with the question of "is this cultural expression genuine?"



\*\*Positive examples (illustrative)\*\*:

\- "Content claiming to represent a religious tradition must accurately 

&#x20; reflect that tradition's teachings."

\- "We may remove content that misrepresents indigenous cultural practices."

\- "Users should not falsely claim affiliation with a cultural or ethnic 

&#x20; group."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT authenticity claims)\*\*:

\- "Misinformation about elections is not allowed." (general authenticity, 

&#x20; not cultural — code under safety only)

\- "Deepfakes are prohibited." (general authenticity, not cultural)

\- "We do not allow impersonation of public figures." (general authenticity)



\*\*Edge cases (HIGH AMBIGUITY — verify in pilot)\*\*:

\- TikTok policy may discuss "misleading content" without specifying 

&#x20; cultural context. Code 0 for this code unless cultural/religious/

&#x20; traditional context is explicitly named.



\---



\### B2. Religious and Sacred Sensitivity



\*\*Definition\*\*: The platform articulates rules about content depicting, 

discussing, or engaging with religious figures, sacred objects, religious 

rituals, or sacred sites — including both protections (against blasphemy, 

mockery) and restrictions (on graphic religious imagery).



\*\*Theoretical anchor\*\*: Religious content has historically been a site of 

contestation between platform free-speech logics and the cultural 

sensitivities of diverse global user bases.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section explicitly references religion, 

religious figures, religious practices, sacred objects, or places of 

worship, in the context of what is allowed or prohibited.



\*\*Positive examples (illustrative)\*\*:

\- "Content that disparages religious beliefs may be subject to reduced 

&#x20; visibility in certain regions."

\- "We do not allow content that mocks religious figures."

\- "Livestreaming inside places of worship requires particular sensitivity."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT religious sensitivity)\*\*:

\- "Hate speech against any group is prohibited." (general safety/hate; 

&#x20; code only if religion is \*explicitly\* named)

\- "Cultural diversity is celebrated on our platform." (community, not 

&#x20; specifically religious)



\*\*Edge cases (HIGH AMBIGUITY — verify in pilot)\*\*:

\- "Sensitive themes" without religious specificity → code 0.

\- Mention of "religion" in a list (e.g., "race, ethnicity, religion, 

&#x20; national origin") \*\*with substantive elaboration in the section\*\* → 

&#x20; code 1; \*\*without elaboration\*\* → code 0.



\---



\### B3. Indigenous and Minority Cultural Protection



\*\*Definition\*\*: The platform articulates specific protections for 

indigenous peoples, ethnic minorities, or marginalized cultural communities 

— recognizing their distinct cultural rights, vulnerabilities, or 

representation needs.



\*\*Theoretical anchor\*\*: Smith (2006) and critical heritage studies 

emphasize that institutional heritage discourse often marginalizes 

indigenous and minority voices; platforms now occupy a similar position 

in determining whose cultural expressions are protected.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section explicitly mentions indigenous 

peoples, ethnic minorities, tribal groups, or specific marginalized 

cultural communities — or addresses cultural appropriation, cultural 

sensitivity, or representation specifically in this context.



\*\*Positive examples (illustrative)\*\*:

\- "We protect content from indigenous creators sharing their cultural 

&#x20; heritage."

\- "Cultural appropriation, particularly of indigenous traditions, is 

&#x20; not permitted."

\- "We work with cultural advisors to review content depicting tribal 

&#x20; practices."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT indigenous protection)\*\*:

\- "We protect users from harassment." (general safety)

\- "Hate speech based on race or ethnicity is prohibited." (general 

&#x20; safety; code only if indigenous/minority \*cultural\* context is named)



\*\*Edge cases (HIGH AMBIGUITY — verify in pilot)\*\*:

\- "Protected attributes including race, ethnicity..." → typically code 0 

&#x20; unless the section substantively elaborates on cultural representation, 

&#x20; not just protection-from-attack.



\---



\### B4. Traditional Craftsmanship and Skill Display



\*\*Definition\*\*: The platform articulates rules or recognition specifically 

for content depicting traditional crafts, artisanal practices, traditional 

performance arts, or skill-based heritage practices (e.g., calligraphy, 

opera, weaving, traditional medicine demonstration).



\*\*Theoretical anchor\*\*: Paquienséguy \& Guo (2025) document how Douyin 

actively curates and promotes ICH-categorized craftsmanship; the question 

is whether TikTok's international policy text addresses such content 

analogously or differently.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section references traditional crafts, 

artisanal practices, performance traditions, traditional medicine, or 

skill-based cultural practices.



\*\*Positive examples (illustrative)\*\*:

\- "Content showcasing traditional crafts is welcome on our platform."

\- "Demonstrations of traditional practices must include disclaimers."

\- "We celebrate creators who share traditional performance arts."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT traditional craftsmanship)\*\*:

\- "Educational content is encouraged." (too general)

\- "We promote creator diversity." (community, not heritage-specific)



\*\*Edge cases (HIGH AMBIGUITY — verify in pilot)\*\*:

\- A section about "creative content" without traditional/craft specificity 

&#x20; → code 0.

\- A section about traditional medicine specifically may also code under 

&#x20; safety (medical misinformation) — code BOTH if both apply.



\---



\### B5. Commercialization of Culture



\*\*Definition\*\*: The platform articulates rules about the monetization, 

commercial use, or commodification of cultural, traditional, or religious 

content — including who may profit from such content and under what terms.



\*\*Theoretical anchor\*\*: A central tension in critical heritage studies 

(Smith, 2006) is between cultural preservation and commercial 

exploitation; on TikTok, this maps onto rules about what cultural content 

is eligible for monetization (gifts, ads, creator fund).



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section discusses monetization, virtual 

gifts, advertising, or commercial use \*specifically in the context of\* 

cultural, traditional, religious, or heritage content.



\*\*Positive examples (illustrative)\*\*:

\- "Content depicting religious ceremonies is not eligible for virtual 

&#x20; gift monetization."

\- "Creators sharing traditional cultural content may participate in our 

&#x20; Creator Fund."

\- "Sponsored content involving sacred objects requires disclosure."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT commercialization of culture)\*\*:

\- "Users may not monetize content that violates copyright." (IP, not 

&#x20; cultural)

\- "Virtual gifts can be sent to any eligible creator." (engagement, 

&#x20; general)

\- "Branded content must be disclosed." (general advertising rules)



\*\*Edge cases (HIGH AMBIGUITY — verify in pilot)\*\*:

\- Generic monetization rules → code 0.

\- Monetization rules applied to "sensitive content categories" without 

&#x20; naming culture → code 0 unless heritage context is clear.



\---



\### B6. AI-Generated Cultural Content



\*\*Definition\*\*: The platform articulates rules about content generated 

or significantly modified by AI that depicts cultural, traditional, 

religious, or heritage subject matter — including AI-generated 

"traditional music", deepfakes of cultural figures, or AI-imitated 

cultural performances.



\*\*Theoretical anchor\*\*: This code captures an emerging governance frontier 

particularly relevant from 2023 onward, as GenAI introduces new forms of 

cultural content production. Connects to Su \& Chan (2025) on GenAI's 

reshaping of platform governance.



\*\*Decision rule\*\*: Code 1 if the section discusses AI-generated or 

AI-modified content \*specifically in relation to\* cultural, traditional, 

religious, or heritage subject matter.



\*\*Positive examples (illustrative)\*\*:

\- "AI-generated content depicting religious figures must be clearly 

&#x20; labeled."

\- "Synthetic media imitating traditional cultural performances is 

&#x20; subject to additional review."

\- "AI-modified depictions of indigenous practices are not permitted."



\*\*Negative examples (NOT AI cultural content)\*\*:

\- "Deepfakes are prohibited." (general AI policy, not cultural)

\- "AI-generated content must be labeled." (general AI labeling, not 

&#x20; cultural)

\- "We use AI to moderate content." (platform's use of AI, not 

&#x20; user-generated AI cultural content)



\*\*Edge cases (HIGH AMBIGUITY — verify in pilot)\*\*:

\- General AI labeling rules → code 0.

\- AI rules that mention "cultural content" or specific cultural categories 

&#x20; → code 1.

\- This code will likely be very rare before 2023; expect most 1-codes 

&#x20; in 2023-2025 documents.

\## Part C: Coding Procedure Notes



1\. \*\*Read the entire section\*\* (3 sentences) before coding any of the 16 codes.

2\. \*\*Code each code independently\*\*: a section can have multiple 1s.

3\. \*\*When in doubt, code 0\*\*: false negatives are preferable to false 

&#x20;  positives in this codebook (especially for the heritage codes, where 

&#x20;  over-coding will dilute signal).

4\. \*\*Heritage codes (B1-B6) require explicit cultural/religious/traditional 

&#x20;  context\*\*. Do not code based on general analogy or implicit relevance.

5\. \*\*For ambiguous sections\*\*: refer to the "Edge cases" notes.

\## Part D: Known Ambiguity Points (to validate in pilot)



The following ambiguity points are predicted to cause coder disagreement 

and require special attention during pilot coding (n = 30):



1\. \*\*Authenticity claims (B1) vs. general misinformation (safety)\*\*: when 

&#x20;  does "misleading content" trigger B1?

2\. \*\*Religious sensitivity (B2) vs. hate speech (safety)\*\*: when does 

&#x20;  listing "religion" as a protected attribute warrant B2?

3\. \*\*Indigenous protection (B3) vs. general anti-discrimination (safety)\*\*: 

&#x20;  threshold for "explicit" indigenous/minority context.

4\. \*\*Commercialization of culture (B5) vs. general monetization rules\*\*: 

&#x20;  how specific must the cultural framing be?

5\. \*\*AI cultural content (B6) vs. general AI rules\*\*: same threshold question.



The pilot phase will explicitly test these distinctions. If agreement on 

any of these points is below 80% between human coder and Gemini 2.5 Pro, 

the codebook will be revised before full-scale coding.
